How to choose SARIMA terms using ACF and PACF

Use ACF and PACF as pattern clues, not rigid rules. Start with candidate orders, then validate with residual diagnostics and out-of-sample performance.

1) PACF cutoff can suggest AR terms (p)

If PACF has strong spikes at early lags and then quickly becomes small, this often hints at a non-seasonal AR process. A rough starting point for p is where PACF appears to cut off.

Visual cue: PACF spikes high at low lags, then fade quickly.

2) ACF cutoff can suggest MA terms (q)

If ACF has strong early spikes and then drops near zero after a few lags, this often suggests non-seasonal MA structure. A rough starting point for q is where ACF appears to cut off.

Visual cue: ACF drops off after a short lag window.

3) Seasonal spikes can suggest P and Q

For monthly data (s=12), spikes at lags 12, 24, 36, ... can indicate seasonal dependence. PACF seasonal spikes often motivate seasonal AR terms (P), while ACF seasonal spikes can motivate seasonal MA terms (Q).

Use the playground to test this intuition by raising spikes near seasonal lags and comparing how your selected parameters match those patterns.

4) Always validate with diagnostics

  • Check residual ACF/PACF to confirm little remaining structure.
  • Compare candidate models using AIC/BIC and forecast error.
  • Test on validation windows; avoid selecting terms only by visual pattern matching.

This tool is for conceptual learning and pattern intuition, not formal model fitting.
